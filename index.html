Introduction

What Analysis Acrobat is for

This app trains one specific exam skill: the extended language-analysis question on 0500 First Language English Paper 1 (Question 2(d) in older papers, 3(d) in the 2027 syllabus). In that question you choose three powerful words or phrases from a short extract and explain how the writer uses language to create an effect, in about 200 to 250 words.

That single question rewards two core moves: explaining the literal meaning of a word or phrase, then going deeper into its implied meaning and effect, all held together by a clear, developed structure. Naming the technique is not directly rewarded by the mark scheme, but we teach it because it sharpens your eye for the writer's choices, gives you the language to discuss them, and carries straight over into your Literature studies. Analysis Acrobat lets you drill each of these skills on its own, then put them together into a full written answer.

The question you are preparing for

Every task looks like this:

Choose three powerful words or phrases from the extract below to analyse how the writer uses language to describe the scene. Write about 200 to 250 words.

You read a short descriptive extract, pick out three of its most vivid choices, and write a paragraph explaining each one. The page shows a complete, labelled model answer.

How the marks are awarded

Examiners judge the quality of your analysis. The mark scheme, simplified:

  • Top bands (4 to 5): you select precise words, explain their literal meaning and go deeper into the associations and effect, showing why the writer chose them.
  • Middle band (3): you choose suitable words and mostly explain their meaning, but your comments on effect stay general.
  • Lower bands (1 to 2): you name a technique, or repeat the writer's words, without explaining the effect.

Examiner reports give the same advice every year: "It is the quality of the analysis that attracts marks." Naming a device is not enough, you must explain how it works. Choose precise words or short phrases, not whole sentences. Depth beats breadth.

How each part of the app builds those marks

  • sharpens your eye for the writer's choices and gives you the vocabulary to discuss them. Naming a device does not earn marks by itself, but spotting it is the first step towards explaining its effect, and the skill carries straight into Literature.
  • trains the two moves: the literal meaning (Move 1) and the deeper, implied meaning and effect (Move 2). Move 2 is exactly what wins Bands 4 to 5, and it is where most students lose marks by stopping too early.
  • drills the full structure: an overall effect, then three ideas, each with a technique, a literal meaning and an implied meaning, so your answer stays complete and well organised.
  • puts it all together: you write a whole answer to an extract and get instant feedback on your choices and structure, plus a model answer to compare against.

Exam strategy: the sentence starters

The opening sentence

The overall effect of the language creates an image of…

Commit to one clear impression: a mood, feeling or picture the whole passage creates. Name an effect, not a technique. For example, "…a town that has been abandoned and left to decay."

Introducing each idea

Firstly / Secondly / Lastly, the writer's use of [technique]…

Take your three language choices one at a time. Name the technique or word class (metaphor, a strong verb, personification…) and embed your quotation inside the sentence.

Move 1: the explicit meaning

…refers to…

Explain what the words literally mean or do: their surface, dictionary meaning. This shows understanding, but on its own it stays in the lower bands.

Move 2: the implied meaning

Additionally / Moreover / Furthermore…

Now go deeper. Explain what the language suggests, hints or makes the reader feel beyond its literal meaning. This connotative comment is where the top marks (Bands 4 to 5) are won.

A full worked example

Here is a complete response to an extract that described an abandoned hotel. Each sentence is tagged so you can see every starter and move in action. Read it straight through first, then look at the labels.

Opening · name the effect

The overall effect of the language creates an image of a once-grand building now abandoned and slowly falling into decay.

Firstly · embed a quotation

Firstly, the writer describes how the sea wind “prowled the corridors like something looking for a room”.

Technique + Move 1 · refers to

The writer's use of personification and simile refers to the way the wind moves freely through the empty, broken hotel.

Move 2 · Additionally

Additionally, the comparison makes the wind seem like a predator or a restless ghost, suggesting the hotel is not merely empty but haunted, and that nothing living belongs there any more.

Secondly · embed a quotation

Secondly, the writer describes how “Paint hung from the walls in long grey tongues”.

Technique + Move 1 · refers to

The writer's use of metaphor refers to the old paint peeling and hanging off the walls in strips.

Move 2 · Moreover

Moreover, calling the strips tongues makes the decay seem almost alive and grotesque, implying the building itself is slowly rotting from the inside.

Lastly · embed a quotation

Lastly, the writer describes how “a door groaned on its hinges”.

Technique + Move 1 · refers to

The writer's use of personification and onomatopoeia refers to the creaking sound the door makes as it swings on its rusty hinges.

Move 2 · Furthermore

Furthermore, the word groaned gives the hotel a weary, human voice, suggesting the building is exhausted and worn down by years of neglect and slow decay.

The pattern repeats three times: quote, name the technique, give the literal meaning (Move 1), then go deeper into the effect (Move 2). The three Move 2 sentences are where the top marks are won.

Techniques explained

These are the eight language techniques you will meet most often in 0500 texts. For each one there is a clear definition, a few worked examples, and a note on how to write about it well. Remember: in the exam, naming a technique earns very little on its own. The marks come from explaining the effect it creates.

Simile

A comparison that uses like or as to say one thing is similar to another. The two things are different, but they share a quality the writer wants you to notice.

as cold as ice

Describes something freezing, or a person who is unfeeling and unwelcoming.

the crowd roared like a hungry beast

The noise is huge and threatening; the crowd seems wild and almost animal.

Going deeper: always ask what quality the comparison transfers. "Her smile was like sunshine" is not about light, it is about warmth and happiness.

Metaphor

Says one thing is another, without using "like" or "as". It is a more direct, more daring comparison than a simile, because it states the two things are one.

the classroom was a zoo

Noisy, chaotic and out of control, full of wild behaviour.

he was drowning in paperwork

Overwhelmed and unable to cope, sinking under the sheer amount.

Going deeper: because a metaphor insists the two things are the same, it often feels stronger and more certain than a simile.

Personification

Giving human qualities, feelings or actions to something that is not human, such as an object, an animal, the weather, or an idea.

the wind whispered through the trees

Whispering is human, so the wind seems gentle, secretive and almost alive.

fear gripped her heart

Fear is treated like a person seizing her, making it feel powerful and physical.

Going deeper: personification is a special kind of metaphor: a metaphor that gives specifically human qualities to a non-human thing. Every personification is a metaphor, but not every metaphor is personification. Because "personification" is the more precise label, use it whenever the human quality is clear.

How comparisons work: tenor and vehicle

Simile, metaphor and personification all work in the same way: they describe one thing by comparing it to another. The thing being described is the tenor; the thing it is compared to is the vehicle. The vehicle lends, or bestows, its qualities onto the tenor.

as cold as ice

The tenor is the person being described; the vehicle is ice. Ice is cold, hard and unfeeling, so those qualities are passed to the tenor.

To analyse a comparison well, name the vehicle and ask: what qualities does it carry, and which of them are being transferred to the tenor? "Her words were daggers" works because daggers are sharp, sudden and wounding, and that pain is given to her words. Spotting the vehicle and its qualities is the fastest route to a strong Move 2. Powerful verb

A strong, vivid action word chosen for its energy or precision, used instead of a plain, ordinary verb.

she sprinted to the door

Far more urgent than "ran": it suggests speed, panic or desperation.

he devoured his dinner

More than "ate": greedy, fast, an almost animal hunger.

Going deeper: a powerful verb can be another technique at the same time. If it gives a human action to a non-human thing it is also personification ("the storm raged"); if it imitates a sound it is also onomatopoeia ("the gate slammed").

Sensory adjective

A describing word that appeals to one of the five senses (sight, sound, touch, taste or smell) to build a vivid picture or atmosphere.

the velvety darkness

Appeals to touch: the dark feels soft, rich, almost comforting.

a bitter wind

Appeals to taste and touch: the wind is harsh, unpleasant, even painful.

Going deeper: always say which sense the word appeals to, and the mood it creates.

Onomatopoeia

A word whose sound imitates the noise it describes, so the reader almost hears the scene.

the bacon sizzled

You can almost hear the spitting, hissing fat.

the gate clanged shut

A hard, metallic, final sound that suggests being trapped or shut out.

Going deeper: many onomatopoeic words are also powerful verbs (sizzle, clang, rustle). The effect is that the reader hears the moment, not just pictures it.

Alliteration

The same opening consonant sound repeated in two or more nearby words.

the wild and windy weather

The repeated "w" adds force and rhythm, almost like gusts of wind.

slippery, slithering snake

The soft "s" sounds imitate the snake's hiss and gliding movement.

Going deeper: alliteration is about sound, not spelling. Its effect is often to draw attention to a phrase or to echo the thing described: soft sounds for softness, harsh sounds for harshness.

Hyperbole

Deliberate, obvious exaggeration for effect, which is not meant to be taken literally.

a mountain of homework

The amount feels impossibly large and overwhelming.

I have told you a thousand times

Exaggerates to show deep frustration at having to repeat something.

Going deeper: hyperbole always reveals a strong feeling, such as frustration, exhaustion or awe. Explain the feeling it exaggerates.

One phrase, more than one technique

Techniques often overlap. "The storm howled" is a powerful verb, onomatopoeia and personification all at once. When this happens in the exam, you do not have to name them all: choose the one technique whose effect you can explain best, and write about that in depth.

Explicit and implied meaning

Move 1 (explicit) = the plain, literal meaning. Move 2 (implied) = the deeper meaning, mood or effect.
The aim of this trainer: to teach you to tell them apart. Choose a level:

Theory: explicit vs implied (tap to revise)

Every strong analysis makes two moves about a quotation:

Move 1: explicit (literal) meaning

What the words actually say or do. The plain, surface meaning you could point to.

Move 2: implied (deeper) meaning

What the words suggest, hint at, or make the reader feel: the mood, the associations a word carries, the effect on the reader. This is where the higher marks are won.

Worked examples

a bruised sky

Explicit: the sky is a dark, discoloured grey.

Implied: a bruise suggests injury and pain, so the place itself feels wounded and full of foreboding.

the pier groaned with each wave

Explicit: the old timber creaks and strains as the waves hit it.

Implied: the groaning makes the whole place feel worn out and barely able to keep going.

his words were ice

Explicit: he spoke in a cold, unfriendly way.

Implied: he wants to push others away and refuses any warmth or closeness.

The most common mistake

Writing a Move 2 that only rewords Move 1. For "his words were ice", saying "his tone was chilly and unwelcoming" is still just the literal meaning in new words. It adds nothing.

The test: if your Move 2 could swap places with Move 1 without losing meaning, it is only a reworded Move 1. Push deeper: what does the word make the reader feel, or reveal about the person or place?
